Welcome to My ENC 1102 ePortfolio

Welcome to my ENC 1102 ePortfolio. My name is David Shontz, and over this semester I built a research project on how Christian devotional websites use motivational, inspirational, and hope-based language and design to reach young adult readers in the United States across the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s. The work in this portfolio is my answer to one question: did I meet the six course outcomes for ENC 1102? My claim is that yes, my writing this semester demonstrates real progress on every outcome, even on the ones I started out afraid of.

This ePortfolio uses a progress argument. I want you to see how far I came from my first reflection in Week 1 to my final research paper at the end of the semester. Use the menu at the top to explore my work. Each page shows one major artifact along with a framing statement that connects the artifact to the course outcomes it demonstrates.

Did I Meet the Six Course Outcomes?

Generating Inquiry

The first outcome is about asking my own real questions about writing, language, literacy, or rhetoric. My research question is the clearest example. I did not just answer a prompt my instructor gave me. I noticed something I actually wanted to know about and I built a whole semester of work around it. My Data Coding Practice is where this inquiry paid off. By coding 39 hope-based items across three real devotional websites, I noticed a pattern I would not have predicted at the start: the kind of hope-based language has shifted across the three decades.

Multiple Ways of Writing

The second outcome is about using multiple modes, languages, or literacies in my writing. This ePortfolio itself is my main evidence. I had to choose colors, layout, photos, fonts, and navigation that fit my research project. My second ePortfolio reflection is also evidence. In that piece, I studied the YouVersion Bible homepage and saw how it mixes written words, photos, video, audio, and layout to reach young adults. My research project itself studies multimodal choices on Christian websites, so this outcome has been a daily part of my thinking.

Information Literacy

The third outcome is information literacy. This is one of the outcomes I really had to grow into. At first I thought any source I found in the UCF library was strong. I learned the hard way that this is not true. While building my Annotated Bibliography, I picked a Christian Century magazine article and got feedback that helped me see why it would not count as a peer-reviewed scholarly source. That weak sample taught me to use the peer-reviewed and full-text filters every time. After that fix, I found stronger sources like Horsfield and Teusner, Williams Gorrell, and Davies and Davis.

Research Genre Production

The fourth outcome is about writing in the genres scholars actually use. I have written several this semester. The Annotated Bibliography taught me MLA hanging indents and how to write source summaries with connections. The Data Coding Practice taught me how to present research data with a coding table. The Practice Claims document taught me how to write a real academic claim with primary and secondary evidence. The Literature Review taught me synthesis instead of summary. The Final Research Paper pulls all of these genres together into one CARS-model article. Each genre has its own rules, and I had to learn the rules for each.

Contributing Knowledge

The fifth outcome is contributing knowledge: drawing my own conclusions from my own data and putting them in conversation with other scholars. My Final Research Paper is the main evidence here. In my Discussion section, I argued that the flavor of hope-based language on Christian devotional websites has shifted across the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s. That claim came from my own coded data, not from a scholarly source. I connected that finding to Dalrymple, Horsfield and Teusner, and Williams Gorrell in the same section. That is what contributing knowledge looks like to me: real data plus real conversation with other scholars.

Revision

The sixth outcome, revision, is the one I feared most. In my first ePortfolio reflection I wrote that writing portfolios were completely new to me and that revising old work felt uncomfortable, because once I turn something in I tend to mentally move on from it. This semester pushed me to do that exact uncomfortable thing every week. My second ePortfolio reflection got revised after I visited the live YouVersion homepage and realized my first draft was wrong about the Verse of the Day placement. My Practice Claims document went from one big claim to two cleaner claims and then got trimmed for word count. My Final Research Paper got revised after I noticed it was too quote-heavy and did not sound like me. Each round of revision has been less scary than the one before.

Looking Back, Looking Ahead

Looking back, the work in this portfolio shows real growth on all six course outcomes. Looking ahead, these skills will keep mattering to me. As I keep studying at UCF and move into my future career, I will keep asking my own research questions, working in multiple modes, checking sources for credibility, writing in different research genres, drawing my own conclusions from real data, and revising my work based on feedback. These are the basic moves of any serious thinker, writer, and researcher. Thank you for visiting my portfolio.
