Generating Inquiry (primary) · Information Literacy (secondary)

Framing Statement

This page shows the process I followed to develop my research question. My first ePortfolio reflection from Week 1 is where I first wrote about Bible devotionals as a genre in my environment. My research methods matching exercise from Week 2 is where I learned which research methods fit which kinds of questions. By Week 4, I had landed on my final research question.

How have Christian devotional websites used motivational, inspirational, and hope-based language; and design elements to reach young adult readers in the United States across the decades of 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s?

Why This Artifact Shows the Outcomes

This artifact shows Generating Inquiry because I did not just answer a prompt my instructor gave me. I noticed something I actually wanted to know about and built a whole semester of work around it. As I wrote in my course outcomes reflection, generating inquiry is bigger than just asking a question. It is about sticking with the question through every part of the research process. This artifact also shows Information Literacy because choosing a good research question depends on knowing what is already out there. I had to read across several scholarly sources to find the gap my question fills.

Instructor Feedback

Comments from Prof. Amy Barnickel (Canvas)

Prof. Barnickel left this comment on my research question proposal (May 20, 2026):

“Hi David. The overall idea for your study is good. The genre examples you have picked are also appropriate for this project. The only thing I would suggest tweaking in your question is to identify the type of language you are going to look at each devotional post for. I would choose about three or four individual devotions per website for your project, rather than trying to focus on the entire website as a whole. You might also want to focus your study on a retrospective or archival aspect of the sites to see how the devotionals have appeared over time.”“For the criteria you want to focus on, choose more specific criteria other than just 'language' as a whole. Specify type of language like 'motivational,' 'inspirational,' and 'hope-based,' for example.”“So your revised question might look something like this: How have Christian devotional websites used motivational, inspirational, and hope-based language; and design elements to reach young adult readers in the United States across the decades of 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s?”“You can use this revised question if you want to, or you can revise again on your own... I think we can get our question approved fairly quickly, and you will also get more points once you have an approved question. — Dr. B”

How I Revised

I used Prof. Barnickel's feedback directly. I specified the type of language I was studying — motivational, inspirational, and hope-based — instead of just “language” as a whole, and I narrowed my focus from whole websites toward specific devotional posts. I also took up the retrospective, across-the-decades angle she suggested, which became the 2000s / 2010s / 2020s frame of my approved final question.
